Cell Division & Cell Cycle — Level A Biology Notes

Cell Cycle Overview

The cell cycle consists of Interphase (cell growth and DNA replication) and M phase (cell division). Checkpoints monitor DNA integrity and spindle attachment.

+ Advanced Details

Interphase: G1: cell growth & protein synthesis; S: DNA replication forming sister chromatids; G2: organelle duplication and preparation for M-phase.

Checkpoints: G1/S: ensures cell health; G2/M: DNA integrity; Spindle checkpoint: chromosome alignment and kinetochore attachment.

M phase: Mitosis or Meiosis occurs, regulated by cyclins/CDKs (MPF). Cytokinesis divides the cytoplasm; animals form a cleavage furrow, plants form a cell plate.

Mitosis — 2 Identical Diploid Cells
Interphase (I)

Growth and preparation for division. DNA is replicated forming sister chromatids. Organelles are duplicated. Checkpoints monitor DNA integrity.

Prophase (P)

Chromosomes condense. Spindle fibers form, kinetochores assemble. Nuclear envelope disassembles.

Metaphase (M)

Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate. Spindle checkpoint ensures proper kinetochore attachment.

Anaphase (A)

Sister chromatids separate after cohesins are cleaved. Chromatids are pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibers.

Telophase (T) & Cytokinesis

Chromosomes decondense, nuclear membranes reform. Cytoplasm divides: cleavage furrow in animals, cell plate in plants.

Meiosis — 4 Haploid Cells
Prophase I

Homologous chromosomes pair (synapsis) forming bivalents. Crossing over occurs at chiasmata, exchanging genetic material for variation. Spindle fibers form, nuclear envelope breaks down.

Metaphase I

Bivalents align at the metaphase plate. Independent assortment occurs, increasing genetic variation.

Anaphase I

Homologous chromosomes separate to opposite poles; sister chromatids remain together. Reduces chromosome number by half (diploid → haploid).

Telophase I & Cytokinesis

Nuclear membranes may briefly reform. Cytoplasm divides, producing two haploid cells. Each still has sister chromatids.

Prophase II

Chromosomes condense again. Spindle fibers form in each haploid cell. Nuclear envelope breaks down.

Metaphase II

Chromosomes align individually at metaphase plate.

Anaphase II

Sister chromatids finally separate and move to opposite poles.

Telophase II & Cytokinesis

Nuclei reform, chromosomes decondense, cytoplasm divides. Results in 4 genetically varied haploid gametes.

Comparison Between Mitosis and Meiosis

Similarities
  • Both are types of cell division
  • Involve chromosome condensation and spindle formation
  • Follow Interphase with DNA replication
  • Ensure genetic material is distributed to daughter cells
  • Both regulated by cyclins and CDKs
Differences
Feature Mitosis Meiosis
Number of divisions 1 2
Cells formed 2 identical diploid 4 haploid, genetically varied
Chromosome pairing No pairing of homologues Homologous chromosomes pair in Prophase I (bivalents)
Crossing over No Yes, during Prophase I
Purpose Growth, repair, replacement Gamete formation, genetic variation
Genetic variation Produces identical cells Genetically varied cells due to crossing over & independent assortment
Chromosome number Maintained (diploid → diploid) Halved (diploid → haploid)
Stages IPMAT Two rounds of division: Meiosis I & II

Scenario-Based Questions

Q1: Cell with sister chromatids, nuclear membrane intact. Stage?

Answer: Late Interphase; next stage: Prophase.

Q2: Plant reproductive cell forms 4 haploid cells. Division & variation process?

Answer: Meiosis; variation from crossing over and independent assortment.

Q3: Homologous chromosomes separate, sister chromatids remain together. Stage?

Answer: Anaphase I of Meiosis I; chromosome number reduced.

Q4: Cells divide for wound repair. Division type?

Answer: Mitosis; produces identical cells for tissue repair.

Q5: Bivalents align at the equator. Division & event forming bivalents?

Answer: Meiosis I; bivalents formed during Prophase I (synapsis and crossing over).
