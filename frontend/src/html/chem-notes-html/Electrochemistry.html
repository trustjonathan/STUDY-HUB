Electrochemistry — A Level (NCDC Aligned)

1. Fundamentals of Redox Chemistry

Electrochemistry is the study of processes in which electrons are transferred between chemical species. Central to this section are oxidation and reduction.

Oxidation and Reduction

  • Oxidation is the loss of electrons.
  • Reduction is the gain of electrons.

Oxidation Numbers

  • Free elements: 0
  • Monatomic ions: ion charge
  • Oxygen in most compounds: –2 (exceptions exist)
  • Hydrogen in most compounds: +1 (metal hydrides: –1)
  • Fluorine: –1 always
  • Group I: +1; Group II: +2

Redox Pairs

A redox pair consists of the oxidized and reduced forms of a species. Example:

  • Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ (oxidation)
  • Cl₂ → 2Cl⁻ (reduction)

Balancing Redox Equations

In acidic medium:

  1. Write oxidation and reduction half-equations.
  2. Balance atoms except O and H.
  3. Add H₂O to balance O.
  4. Add H⁺ to balance H.
  5. Add electrons to balance charge.
  6. Combine and cancel.

For alkaline medium, add OH⁻ to remove H⁺. For neutral solutions, balance without external H⁺ or OH⁻.

Reducing and Oxidizing Agents

  • Reducing agent donates electrons and is oxidized.
  • Oxidizing agent accepts electrons and is reduced.

Comparison using standard electrode potentials:

  • More negative E° → stronger reducing agent
  • More positive E° → stronger oxidizing agent

In displacement reactions, a metal with more negative E° displaces one with less negative (or more positive) E°.

2. Half-Cells and Electrode Potentials

Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)

The SHE is the reference electrode with E° = 0.00 V. It consists of hydrogen gas at 1 atm, H⁺ ions (1 mol dm⁻³), and an inert platinum electrode. Platinum is non-reactive, conducts electrons, and provides a surface for hydrogen adsorption.

Standard Electrode Potential (E°)

Electrode potential arises from electron tendency at the electrode-electrolyte interface. Measured against SHE using a salt bridge and voltmeter. Reduction potentials are tabulated; oxidation potentials reversed in sign. Ion concentration affects potential according to the Nernst equation.

Using Electrode Potentials

A reaction is spontaneous if E°cell > 0.

Calculation:

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode

The higher E° acts as cathode (reduction), the lower as anode (oxidation). E° values rank oxidizing/reducing agents.

3. Electrochemical Cells

Electrochemical cells convert chemical energy into electrical energy.

Types of Half-Cells

  • Metal/metal-ion electrodes (e.g., Zn/Zn²⁺)
  • Gas/ion electrodes (e.g., H₂/H⁺)
  • Inert electrodes (Pt, C) when no metal can conduct electrons

Cell Notation

Written as:

Anode | Anode ion || Cathode ion | Cathode

| = phase boundary, || = salt bridge

Electron Flow

Electrons flow externally from anode to cathode; ions move internally through the salt bridge.

4. The Nernst Equation

E = E° − (0.059/n) log([Products]/[Reactants])

Shows concentration and temperature affect the cell potential.

5. Electrolysis

Molten Compounds

  • Cations migrate to cathode and are reduced to elements
  • Anions migrate to anode and are oxidized

Aqueous Electrolysis

  • Cathode: H⁺ may compete with metal ions
  • Anode: OH⁻ often discharges over other anions, halides may discharge preferentially at high concentration

Concentration, ion type, and electrode material influence products.

Practical Examples

  • Copper purification
  • Electroplating
  • Chlor-alkali production (Cl₂, NaOH)
  • Aluminium extraction from cryolite-Al₂O₃

6. Faraday’s Laws of Electrolysis

First Law

m = (Q × M) / (F × z)

Where m = mass deposited, Q = charge, M = molar mass, F = Faraday’s constant, z = electrons per ion.

Second Law

Masses produced are proportional to molar masses. Q = I × t relates current and time.

7. Conductivity and Electrolytes

  • Strong electrolytes: complete dissociation
  • Weak electrolytes: partial dissociation

Molar conductivity depends on ion mobility and concentration. Conductivity decreases as ions are consumed.

8. Electrochemical Series

Ranks species by reduction potential; used to:

  • Predict displacement reactions
  • Assess reaction feasibility
  • Predict electrolysis products
  • Compare oxidizing/reducing strengths

9. Galvanic vs Electrolytic Cells

Feature Galvanic Electrolytic
Energy conversion Chemical → electrical Electrical → chemical
Spontaneity Spontaneous Non-spontaneous
Electrode signs Anode (–), Cathode (+) Anode (+), Cathode (–)
Purpose Generates current Uses current

Oxidation always occurs at anode; reduction at cathode.

10. Applications

  • Chlor-alkali process
  • Hall–Héroult and Downs cells
  • Fuel cells
  • Batteries (lead-acid, lithium-ion)
  • Electrogravimetry and coulometry

11. Advanced Redox Calculations

Combine half-equations to solve complex stoichiometry. Predict dominant electrode reactions when species have similar E°.

12. Common Exam Challenges

  • Misassigning anode/cathode signs
  • Incorrect reversal of E° values
  • Wrong discharge prediction
  • Unbalanced electrons in half-equations
  • Ignoring physical states
