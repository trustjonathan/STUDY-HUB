A-Level Electrochemistry – Complete Mastery Textbook

1. Foundations of Redox Chemistry

1.1 Oxidation–Reduction Rules

Electrochemistry is entirely driven by electron transfer. Deep mastery requires understanding oxidation numbers, electron flow, and how redox reactions balance in different media.

OIL RIG Logic

  • Oxidation Is Loss of electrons
  • Reduction Is Gain of electrons

Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

  • Elements in their free, uncombined state = 0 (e.g., Na(s), O₂(g))
  • Monatomic ions = charge on ion (Fe²⁺ = +2)
  • Oxygen = -2 (exceptions: peroxides -1, OF₂ = +2)
  • Hydrogen = +1 (metal hydrides = -1)
  • Fluorine = -1 always
  • Alkali metals = +1; alkaline earth metals = +2

Identifying Redox Pairs

Redox pairs show oxidation and reduction half-reactions. Example:

Fe²⁺ / Fe³⁺ is an oxidation pair. Cl₂ / Cl⁻ is a reduction pair.

Balancing Redox Equations

Acidic Medium

  1. Write half-equations
  2. Balance atoms except O and H
  3. Add H₂O to balance O
  4. Add H⁺ to balance H
  5. Add e⁻ to balance charge
  6. Combine and cancel

Alkaline Medium

Same as above, then add OH⁻ to eliminate H⁺.

Neutral Medium

Balance similarly but avoid excess H⁺ or OH⁻.

1.2 Reducing & Oxidizing Agents

  • A reducing agent donates electrons (is oxidized).
  • An oxidizing agent accepts electrons (is reduced).

Strength Using Electrochemical Series

More negative E° = stronger reducing agent. More positive E° = stronger oxidizing agent.

Predicting Displacement Reactions

Metals with lower (more negative) E° displace those above.

2. Half-Cells & Electrode Potentials

2.1 Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)

The reference electrode with E° = 0.00 V used to measure all other electrode potentials.

Why Platinum?

  • Inert and does not react
  • Conducts electricity
  • Provides a surface for H₂ gas adsorption

Why H2 Gas?

Hydrogen ion/electron exchange defines the standard reference.

E° = 0.00 V Because:

It is arbitrarily chosen as reference zero.

Limitations of SHE

  • Hard to maintain 1 atm pressure
  • H₂ gas handling difficulty
  • Electrode poisoning
  • Temperature sensitivity

2.2 Standard Electrode Potential (E°)

How Potentials Arise

Created by electron tendency at the electrode-electrolyte interface.

Measuring E°

Half-cell connected to SHE via a salt bridge → voltmeter measures emf.

Conventions

  • Reduction always written on the right
  • Oxidation always left

Effect of Concentration

Increasing ion concentration shifts equilibrium → changes E according to Nernst equation.

2.3 Using Electrode Potentials

Predicting Feasibility

A reaction is feasible if E°cell > 0.

Selecting Cathode & Anode

  • Cathode = more positive E°
  • Anode = more negative E°

Ranking Oxidizers/Reducers

Order by E° values.

3. Building Electrochemical Cells

3.1 Simple Cells

  • Metal–metal ion (Zn/Zn²⁺)
  • Gas electrodes (H₂/H⁺)
  • Inert electrodes (Pt, C)

3.2 Cell Notation

Example:

Zn(s) | Zn²⁺(aq) || Cu²⁺(aq) | Cu(s)

You must understand:

  • Phase boundaries are denoted by |
  • Salt bridge is ||
  • Anode on left, cathode on right

3.3 Electron Flow Logic

  • More negative E° → More positive E°
  • Anode → Cathode
  • Reducing agent → Oxidizing agent

3.4 Calculating EMF

E°cell = E°cathode – E°anode

Master:

  • Spontaneity (E° > 0)
  • Reversing reactions flips sign
  • Multi-step redox systems
4. Nernst Equation (Advanced Mastery)

E = E° – (0.059/n) log([Products]/[Reactants])

Deep Understanding

  • Concentration affects emf
  • pH affects H⁺ dependent systems
  • E varies due to non-standard conditions
5. Electrolysis (Core + Advanced)

5.1 Molten Electrolysis

Cation is reduced → metal. Anion is oxidized → non-metal.

5.2 Aqueous Electrolysis

Competition at Electrodes

Cathode:

  • H⁺ vs metal ion

Anode:

  • OH⁻ vs halides vs other anions

Concentration Effects

  • Dilute halides → OH⁻ discharged
  • Concentrated halides → halide preferentially discharged

Electrode Materials

  • Inert (Pt, C)
  • Reactive (Cu, Ni)

5.3 Practical Electrolysis

  • Copper purification
  • Electroplating
  • Chlor-alkali industry
  • Aluminium extraction
6. Faraday’s Laws of Electrolysis

6.1 First Law

m = (Q × M) / (F × z)

6.2 Second Law

Mass deposited ∝ molar mass.

You must master:

  • Q = It
  • Moles of electrons = Q/F
  • Multiple electrodes
7. Conductivity & Electrolytes

Strong vs Weak Electrolytes

  • Strong = complete dissociation
  • Weak = partial dissociation

Molar Conductivity

Depends on ion mobility + concentration.

Conductivity During Electrolysis

Decreases as ions are consumed.

8. Electrochemical Series (ECS)

Uses

  • Predict displacement
  • Predict feasibility
  • Predict electrolysis products
  • Rank oxidizers/reducers
9. Galvanic vs Electrolytic Cells
Feature Galvanic Electrolytic
Electron flow Anode → Cathode Cathode → Anode
Reaction type Spontaneous Non-spontaneous
Signs Anode (-), Cathode (+) Anode (+), Cathode (-)
Purpose Generate electricity Use electricity
